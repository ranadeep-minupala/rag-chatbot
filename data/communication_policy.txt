Northwind Robotics — Communication Policy

Overview
Communication is the foundation of an all-remote company. Because Northwind Robotics has no central office and team members work across many time zones, how we communicate determines how well we work together. This policy describes the principles, channels, and norms for communication at Northwind, including expectations around response times, meetings, and documentation. Questions about this policy should be directed to the People team at people@northwindrobotics.com or 555-0188.

Core Principles
Northwind communicates with a handbook-first, asynchronous-by-default approach. We write things down so that knowledge is durable and accessible to everyone, rather than locked in one person's head or in a meeting that others missed. We favor clear written communication over real-time conversation where practical. We share information openly by default, restricting it only when there is a genuine reason. And we communicate kindly, assuming good intent and giving colleagues the benefit of the doubt — especially in text, where tone is easy to misread.

Asynchronous by Default
Most communication at Northwind is asynchronous, meaning it does not require everyone to be online at the same time. Asynchronous communication lets team members work across time zones, protects focus time, and creates a written record. When raising a question or proposing a decision, team members should provide enough context for others to respond thoughtfully on their own schedule, rather than expecting an immediate reply. Real-time conversation is reserved for situations that genuinely benefit from it, such as sensitive discussions, complex problem-solving, or relationship-building.

Choosing the Right Channel
Different kinds of communication belong in different places. Durable information — decisions, processes, and policies — belongs in the handbook or the relevant documentation, so it can be found later. Project and task discussion belongs in the project tracking tool, attached to the relevant work item, so context stays with the work. Quick, informal coordination belongs in chat. Email is used mainly for external communication and formal notices. The general rule is to put information where the people who need it later will look for it, not only where it is convenient to type it now.

Response Time Expectations
Because Northwind is asynchronous, team members are not expected to respond instantly. As a general guideline, team members should respond to messages that are directed at them within one business day, measured in their own working hours. Urgent matters should be clearly marked as urgent and, where truly time-sensitive, raised through a real-time channel. Team members are not expected to monitor or respond to messages outside their working hours, while on approved time off, on a public holiday, or during a company-wide closure. Managers should model and protect these boundaries.

Meetings
Meetings are used deliberately, not by default. Before scheduling a meeting, the organizer should ask whether the goal could be achieved asynchronously. When a meeting is necessary, it should have a clear purpose and an agenda shared in advance, ideally as a collaborative document that attendees can add to. Meetings should be recorded or summarized in writing so that those who could not attend — including team members in other time zones — can catch up. Wherever possible, attendance is optional, and decisions made in a meeting are documented afterward so they do not live only in the memory of those who were present.

Meeting Etiquette
When team members do meet, they should start and end on time, keep cameras and participation according to what makes the group comfortable, and ensure every voice can be heard regardless of seniority or time zone. Team members joining outside their normal hours should be accommodated, and meetings should be scheduled with time zones in mind so the burden of inconvenient hours is shared fairly rather than always falling on the same people.

Writing Well
In a text-first company, clear writing is a core skill. Team members should write so that a reader with less context can still understand: state the conclusion or request early, provide the necessary background, and be explicit about what response or action is needed and by when. Team members should prefer clarity over cleverness, avoid unexplained jargon, and re-read messages from the recipient's perspective before sending. When a written exchange becomes tense or confusing, the right move is usually to switch to a short real-time conversation and then summarize the outcome in writing.

Transparency and Information Sharing
Northwind defaults to transparency. Most information is shared openly so that team members can make good decisions without having to ask. Information is restricted only when there is a clear reason, such as personal data, security-sensitive details, or legally confidential matters. When something must be kept confidential, the reason is usually explained, consistent with the company's value of saying why, not just what. The handling of confidential and personal information is governed by the Code of Conduct and the Information Security Policy.

Inclusive Communication
Northwind's team is global and diverse, and communication should include everyone. Team members should be mindful that colleagues have different native languages, cultural contexts, and working hours. This means writing clearly and avoiding region-specific idioms that may not translate, being patient and generous in interpretation, and ensuring that important information is shared in writing so it does not depend on having been present for a particular conversation. Inclusive communication is a shared responsibility and a practical necessity for working well across the world.
